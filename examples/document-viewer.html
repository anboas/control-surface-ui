53SECTION 1: GENERAL ISSUANCE INFORMATION

541.1. APPLICABILITY.

55This issuance applies to OSD, the Military Departments, the Office of the Chairman of the Joint

56Chiefs of Staff and the Joint Staff, the Combatant Commands, the Office of Inspector General of

57the Department of Defense, the Defense Agencies, the DoD Field Activities, and all other

58organizational entities within the DoD (referred to collectively in this issuance as the “DoD

59Components”).

601.2. POLICY.

61a. The overarching management principles that govern the defense acquisition system (DAS)

62are described in DoD Directive 5000.01 and DoD Instruction (DoDI) 5000.02. The objective of

63the DAS is to implement the national defense strategy, through the development of a more lethal

64force based on U.S. technological innovation and a culture of performance that yields a decisive

65and sustained U.S. military advantage. To achieve that objective, DoD will employ an adaptive

66acquisition framework (AAF) comprised of multiple acquisition pathways. The AAF supports

67the DAS with the objective of delivering effective, resilient, supportable, and affordable

68solutions to the end user while enabling execution at the speed of relevance.

69b. The software acquisition pathway is for the timely acquisition of custom software

70capabilities developed for the DoD. Software programs that meet the definition of a covered

71Defense Business System (DBS) should use the DBS pathway in accordance with DoDI 5000.75

72but may elect to incorporate this pathway for custom developed software.

73c. Programs executing the software acquisition pathway are not subject to the Joint

74Capabilities Integration and Development System (JCIDS), and will be handled as specifically

75provided for by the Vice Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, in consultation with Under

76Secretary of Defense for Acquisition and Sustainment (USD(A&S)) and each service acquisition

77executive.

78d. Programs executing the software acquisition pathway will not be treated as major defense

79acquisition programs even if exceeding thresholds in Section 2430 of Title 10, United States

80Code. See Section 800 of Public Law 116-92.

81e. Programs using the software acquisition pathway will demonstrate the viability and

82effectiveness of capabilities for operational use not later than 1 year after the date on which funds

83are first obligated to develop the new software capability. New capabilities will be delivered to

84operations at least annually to iteratively meet requirements, but more frequent updates and

85deliveries are encouraged where practical. For programs using the embedded software path, this

86annual update applies after initial operational acceptance of the system in which the software is

87embedded and should be aligned with the associated system’s schedule. Before the operational

88acceptance of the system in which the software is embedded, software deliveries will be

89delivered to an operationally representative environment at least annually.

92f. Programs will require government and contractor software teams to use modern iterative

93software development methodologies (e.g., agile or lean), modern tools and techniques (e.g.,

94development, security, and operations (DevSecOps)), and human-centered design processes to

95iteratively deliver software to meet the users’ priority needs. These modern approaches will also

96instrument software such that critical monitoring functions related to the health, security, and

97operational effectiveness of the software can be automated to the maximum extent practicable.

98g. Software development will be done in active collaboration with end users, representing

99key user groups, to ensure software deliveries address their priority needs, maximize mission

100impact, and undergo regular assessment of software performance and risk.

101h. Leveraging existing enterprise services, if available, is preferred over creating unique

102software services for individual programs. These may be procured from the DoD, the DoD

103components, other government agencies, or commercial providers, and leverage category

104management solutions and enterprise software agreements.

105i. Cybersecurity and program protection will be addressed from program inception

106throughout the program’s lifecycle in accordance with applicable cybersecurity policies and

107issuances. A risk-based management approach will be an integral part of the program’s

108strategies, processes, designs, infrastructure, development, test, integration, delivery, and

109operations. Software assurance, cyber security, test and evaluation are integral parts of this

110approach to continually assess and measure cybersecurity preparedness and responsiveness,

111identify and address risks and execute mitigation actions.

112j. Intellectual property (IP) will be addressed from program inception throughout the

113program’s lifecycle in accordance with DoDI 5010.44 and other applicable DoDIs. IP

114considerations will be integrated with, and support, all other program strategies to ensure return

115on government investment and enhance competitive options for development, integration, test,

116deployment, modernization, modular open systems approaches, and product support of software-

117intensive systems.

118k. Software development testing, government developmental testing, system safety

119assessment, security certification, and operational test and evaluation will be integrated,

120streamlined, and automated to the maximum extent practicable to accelerate delivery timelines

121based on early and iterative risk assessments. Maximum sharing, reciprocity, availability, and

122reuse of results and artifacts between the various testing and certification organizations is

123encouraged.

124l. Programs using the software acquisition pathway will report a set of data to the Office of

125the USD(A&S) on a semi-annual basis as defined in the AAF Software Acquisition Pathway

126Guidance located at https://aaf.dau.edu/aaf/software/. Data reported under this pathway will be

127used to monitor the effectiveness of the pathway and will not be used for program oversight.

1312.1. USD(A&S).

132The USD(A&S):

133a. Serves as the decision authority (DA) for special interest programs in the software

134acquisition pathway on a by-exception basis when the size, cost, complexity, performance, joint,

135or congressional interest warrants additional oversight.

136b. Directs acquisition programs to use another acquisition pathway if the software

137acquisition pathway is not deemed appropriate.

139The Under Secretary of Defense for Research and Engineering:

140a. Consults with the USD(A&S) as appropriate on policies and guidance for the software

142b. Guides the development of science and technology activities related to next generation

143software and software reliant systems for the DoD.

144c. Advises the USD(A&S) on software assurance, program protection, developmental testing

145and evaluation, program risks, and other software areas as appropriate.

146d. Advises DoD Components on program planning that anticipates the evolution of software

147capabilities to meet the changing threats, technology insertion, and interoperability including

148addressing technology gaps for DoD programs.

217SECTION 3: PROCEDURES

2183.1. GENERAL PROCEDURES.

219a. A rapid, iterative approach to software development reduces costs, technological

220obsolescence, and acquisition risk. To allocate resources to the most relevant capability needs,

221DoD or DoD component leadership will make software acquisition and development investment

222decisions within a framework that addresses tradeoffs between capabilities, affordability, risk

223tolerance, and other considerations. The software acquisition pathway has two phases: planning

224and execution. Figure 1 outlines key activities and artifacts of the two phases that enable rapid

225and iterative software development and delivery.

226Figure 1. The Software Acquisition Pathway

227b. There are two paths within the software acquisition pathway: applications and embedded

228software. Except where specifically noted, the guidance in this issuance applies to both paths

229equally.

230(1) The applications path provides for rapid development and deployment of software

231running on commercial hardware, including modified hardware, and cloud computing platforms.

232(2) The embedded software path provides for the rapid development, deployment, and

233insertion of upgrades and improvements to software embedded in weapon systems and other

234SECTION 3: PROCEDURES 8

235DoDI 5000.87, October 2, 2020

236military-unique hardware systems. The system in which the software is embedded could be

237acquired via other acquisition pathways (e.g., major capability acquisition).

238c. The DA will document the decision and rationale for a program to use the software

239acquisition pathway in an acquisition decision memorandum.

240d. Existing acquisition programs may elect to update their acquisition strategy to transition

241to the software acquisition pathway or use it in addition to their current acquisition pathway.

242The PM and applicable stakeholders will identify, and the DA will approve, a transition approach

243to tailor processes, reviews, and documentation to effectively deliver software capabilities.

244e. Value assessments will be performed at least annually after the software is fielded to

245determine if the mission improvements or efficiencies realized from the delivered software are

246timely and worth the current and future investments from the end user perspective. More

247frequent value assessments are encouraged if practical.

2483.2. PLANNING PHASE.

249a. Purpose.

250The purpose of this phase is to better understand the users’ needs and plan the approach to

251deliver software capabilities to meet those needs.

252b. Phase Description.

253(1) The planning phase will be guided by a draft CNS developed by the operational

254community. The sponsor will approve the CNS before the execution phase starts.

255(a) The program office and related stakeholders will actively engage users

256throughout the software lifecycle to understand their mission deficiencies, required

257enhancements to existing operational capabilities, cybersecurity requirements, features,

258interoperability needs, legacy interfaces, intelligence needs, threat intelligence, and other desired

259attributes.

260(b) All required capabilities in the CNS should be prioritized to effectively guide the

261software development.

262(c) Periodic review of the CNS should occur at least as often as each value

263assessment to determine if updates are warranted.

264(2) During the planning phase, the DA will select a PM and may establish a new

265program office or assign an existing program office to shape and plan the software acquisition.

266The PM will develop a constrained, tailored set of strategies to acquire, develop, and deliver the

267software capabilities, and will obtain the necessary resources (e.g., people, funding, technology)

268to effectively execute the strategies.

271(3) The program should begin developing the software design and architecture,

272leveraging existing enterprise services as much as possible.

273(a) The program will also consider the development environment, processes,

274automated tools, designs, architectures, and implications across the broader portfolio,

275component, and joint force.

276(b) The chosen software development methodology will incorporate continuous

277testing and evaluation, resiliency, and cybersecurity, with maximum possible automation, as

278persistent requirements and include a risk-based lifecycle management approach to address

279software vulnerabilities, supply chain and development environment risk, and intelligence threats

280throughout the entire lifecycle.

281(c) The program may develop prototypes or initial capabilities to explore possible

282solutions, architecture options and solicit user and stakeholder feedback.

283(4) The DA will validate there are appropriate strategies, analysis, and resources are in-

284place to successfully transition from the planning phase to the execution phase. The approved

285artifacts required to enter the execution phase include the CNS, user agreement (UA), acquisition

286strategy, test strategy, and cost estimate. The DA may have a meeting with key stakeholders to

287review the program’s strategies to make the decision. Programs using the embedded software

288path align their strategies with the programs into which they will be integrated.

289(5) Programs using the software acquisition pathway will be identified in component and

290DoD program lists and databases within 60 calendar days of initiating the planning phase in

291accordance with DoD’s implementation of Section 913 of Public Law 115-91 on acquisition data

292analysis.

293c. Requirements.

294(1) Programs using the software acquisition pathway are not subject to JCIDS, except

295pursuant to a new process as discussed in Paragraph 2.8.a., but must be effective in capturing

296users’ needs, priorities, and environment. The sponsor will oversee development of a draft CNS

297to support the initiation of a software acquisition and use of this pathway. The CNS should be

298clear and concise. Programs using the embedded path will align the CNS with the requirements

299documents of the system(s) the software will be embedded.

300(2) Each DoD Component will create streamlined requirements processes to develop,

301coordinate, and approve the CNS commensurate with the size, scope, risks, threats, and urgency

302of need. The Joint Staff will determine if joint equities are involved and will execute an

303expedited joint validation process if necessary. Insights gained during the planning phase will be

304incorporated into the CNS before approval. The PM will actively engage with the sponsor

305during the CNS development to ensure operational and technical feasibility. The sponsor will

306approve the CNS before entry into the execution phase. Approval of the CNS should be

307delegated to the lowest level practical based on the size, risk, complexity, and interdependency

308of the software needs.

311(3) Current acquisition programs with approved JCIDS documents that transition to the

312software acquisition pathway may continue to use them as the basis of requirements or develop a

313CNS to capture current, software-unique needs. The sponsor will periodically update the CNS

314throughout development as required to reflect the current high-level operational needs for the

315software solution. The sponsor will submit the approved CNS document into the DoD

316Knowledge Management and Decision Support system for awareness and archival.

317(4) Frequent user engagements are critical to the success of modern software

318development to ensure delivered software capabilities address their priority needs.

319(a) The sponsor and PM will develop a UA before the execution phase to gain

320commitment to continuous user involvement and assign decision-making authority for the

324(b) The UA will commit proper resourcing of operational user involvement to

328d. Acquisition Strategy.

329(1) PMs will develop and execute an approved acquisition strategy. The acquisition

330strategy is an integrated plan that identifies the overall approach to rapidly and iteratively

331acquire, develop, deliver, and sustain software capabilities to meet the users’ needs.

332(2) Consistent with modern software development practices, the acquisition strategy and

333related program documentation will be tailored to what is needed to effectively manage the

334program.

335(a) The PM will actively collaborate with program stakeholders and functional

336experts in developing the acquisition strategy given the current environment, priorities, risks, and

337approach.

338(b) The DA will approve the acquisition strategy to include process and

339documentation tailoring.

340(c) The acquisition strategy for an embedded software system must align with and

341may be included as part of the platform acquisition strategy.

342(d) The PM will mature the strategy to the point where it has sufficient rigor for the

343DA to approve beginning development, and will continuously refined it throughout the

344acquisition lifecycle.

2313.1. GENERAL PROCEDURES.

232a. Program Planning.

233(1) A rapid, iterative approach to capability development reduces cost, avoids

234technological obsolescence, and reduces acquisition risk. Consistent with that intent,

235acquisitions will rely on mature, proven technologies and early testing. Planning will capitalize

236on commercial solutions and non-traditional suppliers, and expand the role of warfighters and

237security, counterintelligence, and intelligence analysis throughout the acquisition process.

238(2) Acquisition programs will be designed to facilitate capability enhancements by using

239open systems architectures and common, open, and consensus-based standards. An open system

240design supports sustainment and rapid integration of new or updated subsystems into the

241platform.

242(3) To facilitate a flexible and rapid acquisition process, MDAs, program managers

243(PMs), and other relevant authorities will implement the processes described in Paragraph 3.2.

244b. Decision Reviews.

245The purpose of decision reviews embedded in the acquisition procedures described in this

246section is to carefully assess a program’s readiness to proceed to the next acquisition phase and

247to make a sound investment decision committing the Department’s financial resources.

248Consequently, reviews will be issue and data focused to facilitate an examination of relevant

249questions affecting the decisions under consideration and to allow the MDA to judge whether the

250program is ready to proceed. The policies outlined in Paragraphs (1) through (3) will guide

251decision reviews:

252(1) The MDA is the sole and final decision authority. Staff members and staff

253organizations support and facilitate the MDA's execution of that authority.

254(2) The Defense Acquisition Board will advise the DAE on critical acquisition decisions

255when the DAE, or designee, is the MDA. The DAE or designee will chair the Defense

256Acquisition Board. Similar procedures will be established at the DoD Component level for use

257by other MDAs. An acquisition decision memorandum (ADM) will document decisions

258resulting from reviews.

259(3) Overarching Integrated Product Teams at the OSD level, and similar organizations

260within the DoD Components, are expected to collectively assist the MDA in making sound

261investment decisions for the department, and to ensure programs are structured and resourced to

262succeed. These organizations are not decision bodies and they and their leaders do not supplant

263the authority of the PM, Program Executive Officer (PEO), component acquisition executive

264(CAE), or DAE.

348a. Purpose.

349The MDD is the mandatory entry point into the major capability acquisition process and is

350informed by a validated requirements document (e.g., an initial capabilities document (ICD) or

351equivalent) and the completion of the analysis of alternatives (AoA) study guidance and the AoA

352study plan.

353b. At the MDD Review.

354The Director of Cost Assessment and Program Evaluation (DCAPE) (or DoD Component

355equivalent for ACAT II or below programs) will present the AoA study guidance, and the DoD

356Component will present the AoA study plan. For MDAPs, DCAPE issues the AoA study

357guidance, and approves the AoA study plan. The DoD Component will provide the plan to staff

358and fund program activities up to and including the next decision point, usually Milestone A.

359c. Decisions.

360The MDA will determine the acquisition phase of entry and the initial review milestone.

361MDA decisions will be documented in an ADM. The approved AoA study guidance and study

362plan will be attached to the ADM.

4953.10. MILESTONE B.

496a. Purpose.

497The Milestone B decision authorizes a program to enter into the EMD phase and commit the

498required investment resources to support the award of phase contracts. Requirements for this

499milestone may have been satisfied at the Development RFP release decision point; however, if

500significant changes have occurred between the two decisions that would alter the decisions made

501at the earlier point, those changes will be addressed at the Milestone B review.

502b. At the Milestone B Review.

503This review requires demonstration that all sources of risk have been adequately mitigated to

504support a commitment to design, development and production. Risk sources include, but are not

505limited to, technology, threat projections, security, engineering, integration, manufacturing,

506sustainment and cost risk. Validated capability requirements are required for all programs. Full

507funding in the FYDP, compliance with affordability/program goals demonstrated through

508technical assessments and ICEs are required for MDAPs and programs in other categories when

511The MDA will approve entry into the EMD phase and formally initiate the program by

512approving the acquisition program baseline (APB). The program decisions, EMD phase exit

513criteria, approval of the LRIP quantity, and specific technical event-based criteria for initiating

514production or fielding at Milestone C will be documented in an ADM.
